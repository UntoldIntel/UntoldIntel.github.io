Author’s note to survivor groups

I have written a book about MKUltra. I don’t favour that word, because it seems sensationalist and appears to refer to a different time and place, but it describes the sexual violence and mind control that I experienced.

It is very difficult to find adequate words for such life-altering experiences—it’s perhaps impossible to communicate the scope of these human experience by saying, for example, ‘I’ve been through a war’, or ‘I was raped’. Words, just words: child rape, brainwashing, genocide.

This difficulty of expressing traumatic human experience also applies to mystical vision, or mystical relationship. When I experience my self and the world differently within this relationship, which I call becoming the song rather than the singer, this can be nigh on impossible to express.

It has been the work of my autobiography, Untold Intelligence, to communicate both. On the one hand, the terrible suffering that produced post-traumatic dissociative identity disorder. On the other hand, the wonder of intimacy with divine intelligence. Incidentally, I know that there’s a movement against psychiatric labels like ‘dissociative identity disorder’ in feminist thinking. On Twitter, Dr Jessica Taylor once asked me “How do you have a ‘disordered’ ‘personality’?” I wouldn’t want to argue as I am aligned with protests against victims of abuse being categorised, as such, as ‘mad, bad or dangerous’, and I write about the delusions of the allegation of madness in my book. I also think that this psychiatric terminology has gone some way in usefully describing what happens to the self after trauma, even if there are other ‘languages’ to better talk about this. Referring to the esoteric tradition that I relate to, I say that I write about ‘being after shattering’, and this has enabled me to contextualise my suffering within culture and history rather than seeing it as utterly unique. Even if I am unique!

For many years, I worked in England and Ireland as a human rights lawyer, acting for torture survivors and refugees, but I finally turned to my own story of terror. How, after so much human betrayal and pain, in such a broken world, could there be trust and love within a greater reality? Difficult questions that brought me to write about visiting underground sex clubs, suicide, murder, and the Holocaust.

I’ve told the story of my self—more than my self—being taken from me by referring to all the works of art and literature that have kept me company within terrible loss: Morvern Callar; Stanley Kubrick’s work; books and films by French writer Marguerite Duras. Psychiatrist R. Loewenstein described dissociative identity disorder as “a never-assembled psychological jigsaw puzzle, not a shattered mirror”. 1 Putting together the pieces of my story from years and years of childhood, adolescent and adult diaries was a puzzle but it became prayer. In the writing groups that I facilitate with survivors of child abuse and rape, with my group, ‘Survivors With Biros’, we often practise ‘lament’ and ‘gratitude’ writing. My book is a celebration in great gratitude of being spiritually and erotically awakened, of real relationship, but it is also a lament for terrible shattering, of the self and of the world.

1R. J. Loewenstein, ‘Dissociation debates: everything you know is wrong’, Dialogues in clinical neuroscience, 20(3), (2018), 229–242.
